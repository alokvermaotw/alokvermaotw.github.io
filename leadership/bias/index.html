    Bias

    In the workplace, making incorrect assumptions or evaluations about people can result in a whole set of undesirable behaviours and outcomes. Our often incomplete (or biased) perception can lead us to project expectations of other people’s behaviours (whether positive or negative) and then to come to the wrong conclusion. Considering how quickly we make these associations, it’s important to examine how we make these quick connections and what influences them.

    So, what is unconscious bias? While there is no perfect or all-encompassing definition, we can aim for a general one that you can see below. Also, in this module, we are discussing unconscious social bias – biases that are related to (groups of) people, not objects or places.

    Implicit or unconscious social bias refers to the unconscious attitudes or stereotypes that a person holds about a certain group of people, which influence their attitudes, behaviours and decision-making.

    These biases are formed through our experiences and exposure to cultural messages, and they can operate outside of our conscious awareness, hence us referring to unconscious bias.

    bias involves us making assumptions or quick judgements about others/groups of people. Why and how does this happen? Well, these ‘assumptions’ are, as we said, quick associations that we make between the qualities or attributes we assume some people will have and their appearance or demeanour. Although it might not seem very helpful, making these quick associations actually allows us to navigate life much more easily and not be overwhelmed with too much information.

    It might surprise you to find out that, while we don’t actively ‘feel’ this happening, we are actually constantly bombarded with sensory information that we are unaware we are even processing.

    In fact, it is estimated that we process up to 11 million pieces of information/second unconsciously. To contrast, we can only process up to 40 pieces of information/second consciously.

    1. Why does this happen?
    2. Our brains receive direct input from all of our sensory organs, enabling us to respond rapidly to our senses: to what we hear, see, smell, etc. However, our brains do not have the capacity to process all this information consciously. Even when we just take in our surroundings, it is impossible for us to be consciously aware of everything. What we can do is get the ‘gist’ or 'outline' of a situation.

    objects Environments People
    For example, you know that the image below represents an apple. When you see one, your past experience tells you that it is an apple. You can also estimate how heavy it is, what it feels like, smells like and tastes like, and whether you prefer green or red apples or neither. Imagine how time-consuming it would be to consciously consider each of these things every time you saw an apple. Therefore, being able to make these connections quickly allows us to basically recognise an object almost instantly. However, even though your quick connections would help you categorise apples correctly most of the time, you might be introduced to a fruit that is very similar (in your experience) to apples, when in fact, it is different (like a quince, which is harder, heavier, and tarter). If you had paid close attention (consciously), you might have been able to notice that it was not actually an apple! Similarly, if you were to walk into a large building for a meeting and stroll through a large room that had many desks, screens, and people typing at those screens, you might assume, from your own past experience, that this is an office room of the organisation you are visiting. You might, however, after your meeting, be asked, "What did you think of our library?". Upon closer inspection (that is, by paying conscious attention), you might recognise that the desks are covered in articles and books, and most people seem to be researching or writing documents. Although your assumption was almost correct, by not paying attention, you came to the incorrect conclusion. Let's also consider that you walk past two people talking at a desk, just like in the illustration below. The person who is showing you around the office mentions that the people you see are one of the senior leaders and their executive assistant. From your own experience and from cultural norms, you might already presume: these people look like a man and a woman more women than men work as executive assistants the woman is sitting down while the man is 'talking down' at her few women are in senior leadership positions Because of this, you draw the conclusion that the senior leader is the man and the assistant is the woman Later, however, you find out that the opposite is true. While all of the information before this moment you had was the same as other people around you, your quick assumptions lead you to reach an incorrect conclusion. If instead, you had chosen to consciously investigate what the relationship was, you would not have relied on your assumptions.

    From our examples, it might seem like (unconscious) bias is not a very useful feature of our brains, since it makes us so prone to making mistakes. This is far from the truth. When we're just navigating our lives, bias helps us recognise things quickly and it is most useful in emergency situations.

    If, for example, we notice a fire in a building, we know that the most important thing to do is evacuate and alert others. We have this information from personal experience (fire causes injuries), from education in school or at work (e.g., fire training), and we culturally know that fire is dangerous. Being able to make decisions quickly is essential here.

    When it comes to making important decisions in the workplace, however, such as hiring, promoting, evaluating and developing people, bias is not very good at all.

    All of the activities above, in order to be appropriately carried out, require conscious processing. We explained that we can process much less information consciously than we do unconsciously, and that is because, unlike unconscious thinking, which just recognises and categorises things quickly, conscious thinking involves integrating all of the dispersed information. Therefore, it consumes more energy, so we use it sparingly.

    However, if we become comfortable and assume that every decision we make is correct or unbiased, or if we rely on our 'intuition', what we are really doing is using our biases. As we saw, this often leads us to interpret things incorrectly.

    Making biased decisions or assumptions isn't just relevant because it makes us be 'wrong'. If we allow our biases to remain unchecked or always assume we have made the correct choice, this inevitably leads to people being treated unfairly. This already happens in every industry.

    Because many organisations don't have solid strategies for tackling bias, people are often discriminated against because of their personal characteristics, such as their gender, disability, age, marital status, race & ethnicity, religion, and so on. Even more, if we allow biased behaviour to continue, we are adopting it as a cultural norm in our organisation and making ourselves prone to engage in discrimination, harassment or victimisation, as outlined in the Equality Act 2010.

    Unconscious Precessing/Thinking

    • Underpins unconscious thinking
    • Involves us making associations that help us categorise the world quickly
    • Helps us process the large amount of sensory information in the world.

    Conscious Precessing/Thinking

    • Can only process a small amount of information at a time.
    • intergates information that we are unconsciously aware of.
    • Can help prevent us from making biased decisions.

    WHERE DOES UNCONSCIOUS BIAS COME FROM?

    We have discussed what biases are and the brain mechanisms that underlie how they form, but where do they come from? We mentioned that we form biases from our own experiences, from the experiences of the people around us and from being exposed to cultural influences. What are those cultural influences?

    VALUES, NORMS & KNOWLEDGE

    Systems of oppression Social Norms education
    Any systems designed to hinder a group of individuals from accessing the resources and privileges available to others who are not marginalised. They underpin value systems that create and maintain stereotypes, expectations, prejudice and bigotry. They underlie every aspect of our society, so they are present in every institution and interaction we have. The value systems lead to social norms. The way our societies are structured means that we place more value on some people than others, even though this is not based on any measurable reality. Thus, depending on their characteristics, we expect people to behave in specific (often stereotypical) ways. Our education shapes our perspective on the world. Often, our education, which is not limited to the school setting, has a skewed perspective. This completely excludes the experiences of marginalised people. We should never assume that what we have learned is absolute truth. Instead, we should be open to learning further and reflecting on our perspectives.

    LANGUAGE & THE MEDIA

    Language Media Social Media
    Many people feel that language is just used to describe things, and we might be one of those people. However, research suggests that language actually constructs our reality. Think about the languages that we use, whether that's just English, our mother tongue or any languages we might have learned. Are there gendered words? Are there words that we find 'uncomfortable' or 'insulting'? Much of the media that we consume does not accurately reflect the diversity of experiences that exist in our society. Many rely on stereotypes that are actively harmful. Research shows that, the more media with limited representation we consume, the more these stereotypes build over time and affect our perception of the world. Social media platforms function on algorithmic suggestions. This means that they monitor the content that you engage with (like and comment on), and make similar suggestions. In this way, these apps track our preferences, even if we're not aware of them. Since biases are ingrained in society, marginalised people are not prioritised in these suggestions. Even more, these algorithms also lead us to content that takes biases even further. If we don't examine our beliefs and consume content that is biased, we are likely to be shown ever-more discriminatory material.
    • Where bias comes from - Cultural influences

    Many different sources contribute to how we receive information and form biases and stereotypes. We might think that this has no influence on us. Instead, we might find it insulting that someone might suggest we are biased. Surely, we are not sexist, homophobic, or racist, right? However, because of how our brains predispose us to rely on unconscious thinking and how much information there is around us that reinforces biases, everyone is affected by bias.

    • Having biases is not the problem - being indifferent about them is. We need to actively be conscious of our attitudes and behaviours, so that we can truly be inclusive.
