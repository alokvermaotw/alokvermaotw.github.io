
HAVE YOU EVERY HAD A SITUATION WHERE REALITY DOESN'T MEET YOUR EXPECTATION? 

There might be times when your manager gives you a piece of work and you might feel that you are unsure of how to go about it or if you will be able to meet deadlines. Managing expectations is a key part of managing up so that your manager/client understands how and when they will receive a piece of work.

Part of managing up is avoiding your managers feeling this disappointment with any work they give you. We will go through a few simple steps on how to manage your manager's expectations effectively.

# When given a task, it's always best to clarify the 5 key things: WHO, WHAT, HOW, WHEN, and WHY. 

| Topic | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Who | Do you know who your audience is? Knowing who your audience is will change the output and the language you use so it's good to know this from the start. Is it a senior stakeholder within your own agency? Is it a client? Or is it a group of internal or external individuals? The audience will differ and so will the language and the output of your work. 
| What | Are you sure what you are being asked for? Ask questions to determine whether what you are delivering meets the expectations of your manager, 'Is it just slides or a media plan too? Info in an email?'.  It will save you a lot of time if you deliver what is expected rather than what you believed was wanted. Ask WHAT?
| How | Now that we determined using the topline approach is the way to go. It's best if you explain your topline approach, for example explaining the tools that will be used or what data is needed and where is it going to be taken from. Often discussing the approach will give other people perspective on what your thinking is and they can potentially offer insight into an alternative way of doing things or confirm your thinking. It saves time for everyone. Ask HOW?
| When | Deadlines are super important so make sure you know when your manager needs to see the work and when it is being presented/delivered as they are two different timings. If you don't know the deadline, often the work can slip through the cracks or not get delivered in a timely fashion. Build extra time into your own deadlines to ensure you can deliver on time. Ask WHEN?
| Why | Ask your manager why this piece of work needs to be done. Having an explanation often helps to understand the final goal and creates better outputs. It can also be more motivating and it helps to have clarity. So this isn't taken the wrong way, also explain why you’re asking by asking a simple question - “How does this fit into the bigger picture?”.  Ask WHY? 

# HOW DO YOU COMMUNICATE YOUR PROGRESS

Do you find that other tasks have cropped up and slowed your progress? If so, let your manager know so you can discuss the priority. 

Is there a potential problem that you can see on the horizon? Flag it to your manager so that they have a heads up and aren’t surprised if something doesn’t go to plan. 

Having regular updates on your work, helps them to build their trust in you.

We often tell ourselves 'I can do this or I don't need help and deliver an output that isn't up to our manager's expectations and it can result in a loss of our confidence. Remember that asking for help is ALWAYS okay!

By not asking for help, not giving updates, or even missing a deadline creates uncertainty and that can be a very stressful situation for a manager. 

It's far better for them to help now and correct the direction of your work than for you to plough on and have to make major changes.

# POWER

Power can be defined as “great influence, force, or authority”. Every relationship has power dynamics, and more often than not, the scale is not balanced. Simply put, power is having the capacity or ability to act in certain ways or impose your will on others.

In a work context, people interpret the concept of power in different ways. Some see power as something they receive from an external source. This could be something like a title or position that gives someone control and authority over others.

Others believe power is an innate quality that can be cultivated internally and then manifested externally. In this sense, a person’s personal power grows as they develop. 

What's important to know is that true power is a combination of internal and external power. This means that anyone can access a certain amount of power, regardless of their position.

# POWER AND WORK STYLES
Over the years, many psychologists have researched power dynamics in organizations. John French and Bertram Raven, two social psychologists, completed research on organizational power dynamics(opens in a new tab). Their work shows five different 'bases of power' that may be seen in an organization and used by managers and employees alike. 

We’ve all seen leaders who let power go to their heads. They rule with an iron fist, believing their position gives them the authority to do so. These leaders might succeed in imposing their will on their employees in the short term. But eventually, this management style leads to lower employee engagement and turnover, which is bad for business.

A great leader understands the different types of power and how to use them with influence tactics. Likewise, a great employee understands which power base their manager draws from - and how to best utilize this to their advantage.

## Below are the Five Power Styles you'll see people using. 
| Power Style | Description | Ways to work |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Discusser | The Discusser regularly asks for your opinions on current matters and asks for advice. They need to feel that they are well-liked and respected by those they manage. | Tell them who else has contributed, Agree on when a decision will be made, and whose opinions are required, Frame decisions by providing options |
| The Director | The Director is the quintessential boss. They are good at giving orders, may exclude you from decision-making, and are highly efficient at getting things done. | Make them feel like an expert by asking for their advice, Avoid disagreeing with them in front of others, Keep them informed about everything
| The Operator | The Operator loves to focus on the process. Rules and regulations are highly important to them. As a result, they’re often reluctant to try new innovative new ideas. | Develop detailed project plans, Give examples of how others behaved similarly, Set-up regular review meetings
| The Enthusiast | The Enthusiast is a big-picture thinker, who often forgets the details. They like to have a following. Their thinking is often future-focused, rather than present-day. | Show that you agree with the vision, Be alert and full of enthusiasm, Introduce a structured meeting |
| The Delegator | The Delegator lets you lead. They often ask you for more than is possible, and leave you to make your own decisions. | Ask others for help before going to them, Be sure to clarify what you can and cannot do when you meet, If you have a problem, make sure to present them with a solution |

# WORKING WITH POWER STYLES
Now that you’ve figured out which power styles your manager or other powerful figures in your life exhibits, it’s time to learn how to best work with each style.

When we’re managing upwards, it’s important to acknowledge the elements of the relationship that can’t be changed, as well as to recognize what we do have the ability to control or influence. For example, while you can’t directly control your manager’s mood, you can control the way you react to it. This may in turn influence their behavior, which may mean less frustration for you and possibly some positive recognition from your boss.

# IT'S ABOUT THE BALANCE.
Successfully managing upwards means appreciating the other person’s natural tendencies. However, if someone is unreasonable, we shouldn’t tolerate it. Harvard organizational management professor Linda Hill’s research(opens in a new tab) explains how avoiding conflict is not always the way to gain respect and reminds us that there is a mutual dependency in the employee-manager relationship.
