# Bias
In the workplace, making incorrect assumptions or evaluations about people can result in a whole set of undesirable behaviours and outcomes. Our often incomplete (or biased) perception can lead us to project expectations of other people’s behaviours (whether positive or negative) and then to come to the wrong conclusion. Considering how quickly we make these associations, it’s important to examine how we make these quick connections and what influences them.

So, what is unconscious bias? While there is no perfect or all-encompassing definition, we can aim for a general one that you can see below. Also, in this module, we are discussing unconscious social bias – biases that are related to (groups of) people, not objects or places.

`Implicit or unconscious social bias` refers to the unconscious attitudes or stereotypes that a person holds about a certain group of people, which influence their attitudes, behaviours and decision-making.

These biases are formed through our experiences and exposure to cultural messages, and they can operate outside of our conscious awareness, hence us referring to unconscious bias.

bias involves us making assumptions or quick judgements about others/groups of people. Why and how does this happen? Well, these ‘assumptions’ are, as we said, quick associations that we make between the qualities or attributes we assume some people will have and their appearance or demeanour. Although it might not seem very helpful, making these quick associations actually allows us to navigate life much more easily and not be overwhelmed with too much information.

It might surprise you to find out that, while we don’t actively ‘feel’ this happening, we are actually constantly bombarded with sensory information that we are unaware we are even processing. 

In fact, it is estimated that we process up to 11 million pieces of information/second unconsciously. To contrast, we can only process up to 40 pieces of information/second consciously.


1. Why does this happen?
- Our brains receive direct input from all of our sensory organs, enabling us to respond rapidly to our senses: to what we hear, see, smell, etc. However, our brains do not have the capacity to process all this information consciously. Even when we just take in our surroundings, it is impossible for us to be consciously aware of everything. What we can do is get the ‘gist’ or 'outline' of a situation.

From our examples, it might seem like (unconscious) bias is not a very useful feature of our brains, since it makes us so prone to making mistakes. This is far from the truth. When we're just navigating our lives, bias helps us recognise things quickly and it is most useful in emergency situations. 

If, for example, we notice a fire in a building, we know that the most important thing to do is evacuate and alert others. We have this information from personal experience (fire causes injuries), from education in school or at work (e.g., fire training), and we culturally know that fire is dangerous. Being able to make decisions quickly is essential here. 

When it comes to making important decisions in the workplace, however, such as hiring, promoting, evaluating and developing people, bias is not very good at all. 

All of the activities above, in order to be appropriately carried out, require conscious processing. We explained that we can process much less information consciously than we do unconsciously, and that is because, unlike unconscious thinking, which just recognises and categorises things quickly, conscious thinking involves integrating all of the dispersed information. Therefore, it consumes more energy, so we use it sparingly. 

However, if we become comfortable and assume that every decision we make is correct or unbiased, or if we rely on our 'intuition', what we are really doing is using our biases. As we saw, this often leads us to interpret things incorrectly. 

Making biased decisions or assumptions isn't just relevant because it makes us be 'wrong'. If we allow our biases to remain unchecked or always assume we have made the correct choice, this inevitably leads to people being treated unfairly. This already happens in every industry. 

Because many organisations don't have solid strategies for tackling bias, people are often discriminated against because of their personal characteristics, such as their gender, disability, age, marital status, race & ethnicity, religion, and so on. Even more, if we allow biased behaviour to continue, we are adopting it as a cultural norm in our organisation and making ourselves prone to engage in discrimination, harassment or victimisation, as outlined in the Equality Act 2010.

## Bias in Society
- This translates into people being treated differently, and more often than not, unfairly. This happens simply because of who they are - aspects of their identities that they cannot change. When we show preferential treatment to people that are similar to us or that meet our expectations of what we think is 'desirable' or 'good', we are actually being influenced by our biases. At the same time, we are excluding (or discriminating against) others.

### In the UK, it is unlawful to discriminate against anyone on the basis of 9 protected characteristics, which are outlined in the Equality Act 2010.  

| Type | Description |
| --- | --- |

| Sex | This characteristic refers to a person's binary gender (women and men), protecting against sexist discrimination.|

# UNCONSCIOUS BIAS: Cultural influences
Biases come from cultural influences that create stereotypes. There are many sources - where we live, what we learn, what media we consume. These social norms and expectations influence all of us, even though we are not aware. Unconscious bias impacts everyone. 

# IMPACTS: Bias in the workplace
Because bias is fundamentally part of society, we are affected in the workplace, as well. Bias impacts who gets hired and promoted, who receives good feedback, who gets opportunities, and who can feel that they belong. When we don't take action to minimise or eliminate bias, we lose out on talent, underperform, and our community suffers.

# IMPACTS: Bias and managers
As people managers, bias is especially important. People's relationship with their manager is defining for their experience in the workplace. This means that we have increased responsibility. This is also a great opportunity to model positive behaviours and support others with working inclusively.

# CONSCIOUS INCLUSION: Personal commitments
Combating biases requires personal commitment. We need to actively seek to learn more about others, reflect on our own behaviour, use inclusive language and change our behaviour. This personal commitment is necessary for changing our culture.

# CONSCIOUS INCLUSION: Working together
Changing our culture, however, can only happen if we work together. We need to examine the way that we work and assume that bias is already present. Then, we can design with inclusion in mind, working collaboratively and holding ourselves accountable. In this way, we find innovative solutions that improve everyone's experiences. With collaboration, we can change our culture to an inclusive and welcoming environment, where everyone belongs.
