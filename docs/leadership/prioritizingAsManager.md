# TIME MANAGEMENT

For centuries, people have been looking for ways to manage their time. Many theories on how to best manage your time have evolved over the years. These theories are still relevant today.  

We’ll look at two time management theories: The Eisenhower Matrix and Stephen Covey’s Rocks and Gravel that we can apply in our day-to-day lives to improve our time management.

## METHOD ONE: THE EISENHOWER MATRIX

Dwight D. Eisenhower was the 34th president of the United States from 1953-1961. President Eisenhower became renowned for his ability to manage competing tasks, and eventually developed a method for doing so called “The Eisenhower Matrix.” His method helps us to prioritize tasks by urgency and importance. 

!https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tT89OZ7TNwc


| **Eisenhower Matrix** | important | Less Important |
| --- | --- | ---|
| Urgent | Things to do immediately - deliver a presentation to a client | Things that are important but can be rescheduled - demonstrating a new system
| Not Urgent | Vital for your goals but not time critical - reading a work related book | Thing that distract you without a benefit - surfing the internet
| 

This matrix probably works best as a mental framework rather than an actual physical framework you fill out and use, but that's up to you. 

''What is important is seldom urgent and what is urgent is seldom important.'' - Dwight D. Eisenhower

## METHOD TWO: STEPHEN COVEY’S ROCKS AND GRAVEL
Stephen Covey was an American businessman, educator, and public speaker. He was famous for his work in time management in the workplace and wrote the book The Seven Habits of Highly Effective People(opens in a new tab) in 1989, which is still a best-selling book today.

Covey’s work includes a method he called “Rocks and Gravel” that helps us to understand what our most important tasks are (rocks), vs. our less important work (gravel). Watch the video below to learn more about Covey’s theory of “Rocks and Gravel.”


| Tasks | Descriptions |
| --- | --- |
| Big Rocks First | Your important tasks are your “big rocks.” They come first!
| Set clear Goals | For your four-five big rocks, make sure you set clear goals. Be honest with yourself about how you will measure success.
| Share your big rocks | Make sure to share your “big rocks” with the team. Depending on the situation, you can also share your big rocks with key clients and stakeholders.
| Schedule your big rocks | As they are your priority, make sure you schedule your big rocks/important tasks into your calendar first.
| Don't schedule your gravel | Instead, let your gravel fall into place after your big rocks have been calendered.
|

# DELEGATION
## DON'T DO, LEAD 
Team management does not mean that you do all of the work yourself. Doing is not synonymous with leading. The art of leadership is how you get work done through others. You’re supporting your team with what they need to succeed, and empowering them to do their best work to achieve shared goals. 

That’s why delegation is an essential team management skill. While delegating tasks to others may feel counterintuitive, in this lesson, you’ll learn why effective delegation is critical to productivity, team development, and even team resiliency.

## WHAT IS DELEGATION?
Delegation is an essential skill for a manager and it means becoming an orchestrator for your team.

You are there to ensure things get done, not do them yourself. Delegation isn’t about abdicating responsibility for the task but allows you to entrust others with authority to perform the activity or achieve the goal on your behalf.

So do take the time to identify each of your team members' strengths, passions and professional development goals. Then delegate tasks and structure projects in a way that motivates them intrinsically and contributes to their goals.

Delegation should be a positive activity that supports and encourages. It can develop the skills, confidence and commitment of your team and free up your team to focus on other things.

### THERE ARE MANY ADVANTAGES OF DELEGATION. AND THEY INCLUDE:

1. It makes time for you to concentrate on your priorities and goals
2. It develops the team by exposing them to new opportunities, increasing their responsibilities, abilities, morale and motivation

3. It increases efficiency and flexibility within the team
4. Helps you get to understand their strengths and weaknesses and who is ready take on more challenging roles
5. It drives increased motivation, retention, and trust.

### REASONS WHY PEOPLE DON'T DELEGATE AND HOW TO OVERCOME IT
- There are various reasons you might be reluctant to delegate. Have a think about which of these most resonate with you. 

1. Step 1. YOU DON'T TRUST THEM

    - Common Slogans: “I can do it better myself.” Or, “I’m afraid that they won’t get it done/right.”

    - You will never be able to trust your team if you don’t give them the opportunity to rise to the challenge, demonstrate their competencies, and prove their skills. Start small. 

    - Give your team a chance to take responsibility for smaller tasks before gradually building trust and working your way up to delegating more significant responsibilities. 

2. Step 2: IT'S QUICKER TO DO IT YOURSELF
    - Common Slogans: “It will be faster just to do it myself.” Or, “I’m too busy to teach someone else.”
    - Accept that dividing the work between multiple people, as opposed to doing all of the work yourself, is a more efficient way to manage workloads.

3. Step 3: I FEEL GUILTY

    - Common Slogans: “I want my team to like me.” Or, “Everyone’s already so busy, I don’t want to dump this on their plate, too.”
    - Reframe delegation as a team benefit—not a burden. New responsibilities provide your team with opportunities to try new things, develop or practice new skills, and advance their careers. That’s exciting! Delegating can also lead to higher levels of team engagement. 
4. Step 4: YOU DON'T WANT TO LOSE CONTROL
    - Common Slogans: “I want to make sure it’s done right.” Or, “I like to feel in control.”
    - Delegating work to others doesn’t mean that you have to let go of all control. Establish clear boundaries, define clear objectives, and feel empowered to set any other parameters, priorities, or check-in meetings necessary for your team’s success. 
5. Step 5: YOU ENJOY THE WORK
    - Common Slogan: “I enjoy doing this work, and I don’t want to give it up.”
    - Delegation is about sharing and dividing tasks. It’s okay if you take on some of the work that you enjoy. However, this could become a problem if doing the work, you enjoy most interferes with your ability to manage others. 

You may find it difficult to stop old habits and you may want to jump into the work alongside your team or fix or even redo what they’ve completed. This isn’t sustainable and you need to guide your team to reach the goals so they can work autonomously.

## Focus on your team, not yourself. 

Notice how many of the reasons people don’t delegate involve an “I” statement or otherwise revolve around the team manager’s perceptions and feelings? When you shift the focus away from how you’re feeling, and concentrate on what’s best for your team instead, you may find it easier to let go and start delegating.

# RIGHT JOB, RIGHT PERSON
DELEGATION IS A SKILL, AND IT GETS EASIER AS YOU PRACTICE 

DELEGATION CHOICES

The first question you may have is, “How do I know which tasks to delegate to others, and which ones are better to do myself?” Think about how to divvy up tasks in a way that will balance and maximize the benefits to you, your team, and your company.

| What to Delegate | What Not to Delegate |
| --- | --- |
| Tasks that would train your team and develop their skills | Tasks that are personal or confidential|
| Tasks that are better suited to others’ experience, knowledge, or skill sets | Tasks that involve legal matters or could have legal ramifications
| Low-priority tasks that are taking your time away from high-priority items  | Tasks assigned exclusively or explicitly to you
| Tasks that fit your team’s interests  | Tasks that your team isn’t capable of performing | 
| Tasks that your team could easily handle | Critical business tasks that have little room for error or demand your close attention and control
| Tasks that would challenge your team, and allow room for mistakes or extra time for feedback and revisions | Tasks that require a level of decision-making and risk that may be too much for your team Tasks that need your expertise and leadership

## HOW TO CHOOSE THE RIGHT PERSON FOR THE JOB 
### STEP ONE: KNOW YOUR NEEDS

The first step in delegating the right tasks to the right people is to know your needs. Evaluate the task and its needs. What’s involved? How complex is it? What special skills, knowledge, or abilities are required? And are there any other constraints worth considering? Flip the following flashcards to get to know your task needs:

| Topic | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Requirements | Consider what specialized skills, knowsledge, or abilities are required to succeed in this task |
| Deadlines | Examine the task deadlines and time constraints. Is it time-sensitive? Are the task's deadlines flexible or fnflexible?
| Room For Errors | Identify room for error. For Example, A client-facing or public-facing document may have less room for error than an internal document |
| Complexity | Also, consider the task's complexity. Does it have clear steps that a team member could follow? Or will it require some navigation and a higher level of decision-making.

### STEP TWO: KNOW YOUR TEAM 

Next, you need to know your team. Get to know your team members as individuals so that you can better understand their skills, strengths, gaps, and professional goals. You also need to consider any practical constraints on their time and priorities. 

### STEP THREE: MATCH TASKS TO TEAM MEMBERS 

Finally, now that you know your needs and know your team, you can match each task to the appropriate team member effectively. For example, you might match an especially creative, abstract, or unique task to a more seasoned team member. They’ll have the confidence and experience to work through the task’s ambiguity, and the challenge will help to re-engage and excite them at work. Or, you might assign a task that has a flexible deadline to a newer or less-experienced employee. You can use the task as a training opportunity since there’s plenty of room for feedback and error.

1. Do you do your best work in the morning, afternoon or evening?

## MULTITASKING

Multitasking is the idea that a person can split their attention on more than one task at a time. 

Does multitasking make you more productive?  Let’s take a closer look.

### DOES MULTI-TASKING WORK?

The truth is, our brains can only focus on one task at a time. To do something well, we need to focus our full attention on it.

Multitasking may work when we are doing more routine tasks, such as driving or talking to the person in the passenger seat. But when it comes to multitasking on high-concentration tasks - like holding a conversation and writing at the same time - both your writing and conversation will suffer.

### WHAT DOES THE RESEARCH SAY?

Research has shown that 98% of the population can’t multitask well, and that multitasking can reduce productivity as much as 40%. [learn more](https://www.forbes.com/sites/lisaquast/2017/02/06/want-to-be-more-productive-stop-multi-tasking/?sh=63b166ec55a6).

### WHAT CAN I DO ABOUT IT?

Put away your laptop and mobile phone during meetings to give others your full attention. If you’re holding a meeting, ask others to do the same.

### HOW CAN YOU MANAGE YOUR MIND?

Practicing mindfulness has proven to help with multitasking. When you find yourself getting overwhelmed, stop what you’re doing and close your eyes. Take a deep breath and hold it for a count of five. Then slowly release your breath along with all your stress and worries. Do this three times.

### PROCRASTINATION

Almost everyone procrastinates to some degree. In fact, according to a recent study, 88% of the workforce admits that they procrastinate at least one hour a day [learn more](https://medium.com/darius-foroux/how-common-is-procrastination-a-study-80869467c3f3).  It’s a giant problem!

Obviously, the benefits of not procrastinating include getting more done and higher productivity. But did you know there are additional benefits to not procrastinating? Click below to learn more.

#### LOWER STRESS AND ANXIETY
Research has shown(opens in a new tab) that the longer we wait to get started on something, the more stress, and anxiety we have. If instead, you do something now, you remove the anxiety before it gets to you.

#### HIGHER SELF DESCIPLINE
Interestingly, one of the biggest reasons people procrastinate is not only poor time management skills, but also because of poor self-discipline. Stopping the cycle automatically raises your self-discipline.

#### BETTER WORK OUTPUTS
Researchers also found that when students worked with less stress and anxiety and higher self-discipline, more feedback is sought, and their work output is better.

## TIPS FOR REDUCING PROCRASTINATION

There are several techniques that people have used to stop procrastinating. Most of them involve having daily habits and systems that prevent you from veering off on a path of unproductiveness. Click the “+” signs below to learn more about reducing your procrastination.

### START YOUR HARDEST TASK FIRST

Mark Twain famously called completing the unpleasant things first thing in your day “eating the frog”. The thought is that once you eat that frog, you’ll know that the worst is behind you.

### HAVE A SET OF DAILY HABITS

Maybe these habits are journaling, setting daily priorities, reading the news, or making sure that you interact with friends and family every day. You’ll have more control of your life by developing a series of daily habits in your day.

### [EXERCISE YOUR MIND](https://ccsuconed.wordpress.com/2017/01/23/the-top-7-benefits-of-learning-a-new-skill/)

Learning new skills is good exercise for your mind(opens in a new tab). It’s been found to stimulate neurons in the brain. Learning also increases the density of your myelin, or the white matter in your brain that helps improve performance on a number of tasks.

### [EXERCISE YOUR BODY](https://www.emerald.com/ijwhm/article-abstract/1/3/176/161011/Exercising-at-work-and-self-reported-work?redirectedFrom=fulltext)

Even if it’s only a few minutes a day, make it a habit. Research has shown that exercise not only improves well-being, but also improves productivity(opens in a new tab). In fact, participants noted a 72% improvement in time management and workload completed on days when they exercised.

### HAVE A SMALL LIST OF TASKS TO DO!

Always have a list of small, yet important tasks to complete. If there’s a large, daunting task ahead of you (and you’re convinced today is not the day to do it), your ‘small task’ list will help you turn to something else productive.
